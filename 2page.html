The Historic Campaign

After returning to the throne in 1451, Fatih Sultan Mehmed II turned his full attention to the centuries-old dream of many Muslim rulers: the conquest of Constantinople. The city, capital of the Byzantine Empire, had stood as a symbol of Christian power for over a thousand years and blocked Ottoman expansion.

Mehmed prepared meticulously for two years. He built a powerful new fortress called Rumeli Hisarı on the European side of the Bosphorus to control sea traffic. He cast enormous cannons, including the famous Basilica cannon designed by the Hungarian engineer Urban, capable of firing massive stone balls that could shatter the ancient Theodosian Walls.

On April 6, 1453, at the age of only 21, Mehmed II began the siege with an army of approximately 80,000–100,000 men, a powerful navy, and revolutionary gunpowder artillery. The Byzantine defenders, led by Emperor Constantine XI, numbered fewer than 7,000 soldiers and relied on the massive walls and the Golden Horn chain for protection.

After weeks of intense bombardment, Mehmed achieved a brilliant tactical move: he transported dozens of ships over land from the Bosphorus to the Golden Horn, bypassing the chain. This shocked the defenders and allowed Ottoman ships to threaten the city from inside the harbor.

On the night of May 28, Mehmed addressed his troops with determination, saying:

"Either I conquer Constantinople or Constantinople conquers me."

The final assault began before dawn on May 29, 1453. After hours of fierce fighting, the Ottoman forces broke through the walls near the Gate of St. Romanus. Constantinople fell after 53 days of siege. Mehmed entered the city as conqueror, rode to Hagia Sophia, and declared it a mosque — marking the end of the Byzantine Empire and the beginning of a new Ottoman era.

This victory transformed Mehmed into Fatih ("the Conqueror") and made Istanbul the new capital of a rapidly expanding empire.
